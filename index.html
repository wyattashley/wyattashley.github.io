2022 FIRST Robotics

Intake

 In the realm of robotics, the Swerve Chassis represents a significant innovation that seamlessly combines precision, agility, and versatility. This technology incorporates four rotational and translational axes, introducing complexity when configuring the forward and reverse kinematic equations.

 My primary responsibility within the project was to develop these essential equations, ensuring that with just three input commands (left/right, forward/reverse, and turning), all four wheels operate in harmony. This experience underscores my capability to address intricate engineering challenges and contribute to the advancement of robotics.

 For this particular season, our team made the strategic decision to incorporate a two-wheel launcher into our setup, offering us the capability to apply precise amounts of spin to the game ball. In my role, I took charge of prototyping, contributed to the design process, and successfully programmed the control system responsible for launching the ball.

 By implementing variable spin and adjustable launch distances, I was able to create an adaptive launcher that proved to be highly effective in scoring the ball from any position on the field. This project highlights my skills in both hardware development and programming.

 Due to the fact the game piece being a sizable ball, our team collectively opted for a two-stage compliant wheel intake mechanism. In the initial stage, the ball was taken in by our primary intake, after which it proceeded to a wheeled condenser. This secondary stage was responsible for positioning the ball more predictably, preparing it for a more accurate launch.

 This decision demonstrates thoughtful approach to game strategy and ability to devise a well-coordinated, multi-stage solution to address the challenges posed by the game piece.

Oxford T-Shirt Cannon

Version 1

 One of the most significant undertakings during my tenure on the team was the design and construction of an Air-Powered T-Shirt Cannon. Taking charge of the entire design process, our initial version was essentially a prototype, serving as the foundation upon which we would refine the final product.
 In the image, you can see the first version of our T-Shirt cannon, which employed a single air-tank system equipped with dual air-compressors. The design included smaller secondary storage tanks, each attached to individual cannons, pressurized to varying levels for different shooting distances. A key component was a 2-inch sprinkler valve, which was controlled to release the compressed air.
 To ensure optimal performance, the T-Shirt cannon's drive train was constructed using durable stainless steel chains connected to a planetary gear set. This dynamic setup was driven by two independent Falcon motors on either side, delivering an abundance of power.

Version 2

 In this image, you'll see the second iteration of our T-Shirt cannon, which underwent significant improvements. We incorporated a dual air-tank system, increasing the reservoir's capacity for enhanced performance.
 For this second version, we improved stability by replacing the vertical supports with sturdy 1.5-inch aluminum A-Frame supports. Moreover, we introduced eye-catching LED panels that wrapped the robot's exterior, offering advertising space. The machine's power source comprises two 60Ah car batteries, efficiently driving the drivetrain while separately powering the display panels and compressor.
 During the second-day field test, the T-Shirt cannon showcased its capabilities with an impressive maximum range exceeding 192 feet, hitting the wall at a height of over 20 feet off the ground. This project serves as a testament of expertise in design, programming, and engineering.

Mechanical

 The kart design featured three wheels in the hopes of reducing inefficient turning without the use of a differential on the back axle. Furthermore, we created a fully aluminum chassis and roll cage to reduce as much weight as possible for a longer runtime. Yet another feature on the kart was an adjustable seat to ensure that everyone on the team could fit inside the kart. Finally, the suspension consisted of two independent shocks for the front wheels and two parrallel shocks for the rear wheel.
